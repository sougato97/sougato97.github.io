## Abstract

US healthcare is not one system with one decision-maker. It is a network of
patients, providers, payers, employers, regulators, drug companies, device
companies, and intermediaries, each operating with different constraints and
incentives.

This draft is a working map of those stakeholders, what each of them controls,
and where the biggest points of friction show up.

## Patients

Patients are the people the system is supposed to serve, but they are often the
least informed party in the transaction. They face fragmented pricing,
asymmetric information, administrative complexity, and uneven access.

## Providers

Hospitals, physician groups, clinics, and independent practitioners deliver
care. They balance clinical outcomes, staffing, reimbursement, compliance, and
operational efficiency.

## Payers

Payers include private insurers as well as public programs such as Medicare and
Medicaid. They shape access through coverage rules, prior authorization,
network design, and reimbursement structures.

## Employers

Employers influence healthcare more than many people realize because they fund
or sponsor a large share of insurance coverage in the US. They care about cost,
access, productivity, and employee retention.

## Pharma And Biotech

Drug companies and biotech firms drive discovery, trials, commercialization,
and pricing decisions around therapies. Their incentives are tied to R&D risk,
patent windows, market access, and reimbursement.

## Medical Device Companies

Device makers shape diagnosis, monitoring, procedures, and care delivery
infrastructure. Their role intersects with providers, regulators, and payers in
different ways than therapeutics do.

## Regulators And Public Agencies

Agencies such as the FDA, CMS, and state-level bodies define the rules of the
system. They affect safety, reimbursement, reporting, privacy, and market
entry.

## Intermediaries

Pharmacy benefit managers, clearinghouses, revenue-cycle vendors, and other
administrative intermediaries shape how money and information move through the
system. They often sit in the gap between what care costs, what gets billed,
and what gets paid.

## Why This Map Matters

A lot of healthcare problems are not purely technical or purely clinical. They
are coordination problems across stakeholders with different objectives. If that
stakeholder map is unclear, it becomes harder to design products, workflows, or
AI systems that actually hold up in production.
